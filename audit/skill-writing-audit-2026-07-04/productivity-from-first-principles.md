## Failure-mode test appears to contradict the convergence carve-out

**Gap:** Line 17 says converging on the standard approach "is fine, but only because the fundamentals forced it there." Line 21's failure-mode test says "if the reasoning never discards an assumption or reaches a non-obvious conclusion, the mode was performed, not used." Taken literally, a legitimate run that interrogates every list-(b) item and honestly finds they all hold (discarding nothing, landing on the conventional answer) would pass line 17's carve-out but fail line 21's test — the two statements disagree on whether an all-survive outcome is genuine or relabeling.

**Why this is a tradeoff, not a clear-cut fix:** Resolving this requires deciding what the failure-mode test actually checks — process (was every item seriously interrogated?) versus outcome (did anything get discarded or was the conclusion non-obvious?). That's a substantive change to the skill's verification criteria, not a wording/terminology fix. It also touches process logic (the failure-mode heuristic) that may have been written this way deliberately, e.g. to keep the bar high by requiring some visible sign of work rather than trusting a self-report that "I checked everything and it all held."

**Recommendation:** Adopt the suggested rewrite — make the test check whether list (b) items were actually interrogated ("is it a law of the domain, or just convention?") rather than whether anything was discarded. This makes an honest all-survive outcome pass the test as long as the interrogation happened, resolving the contradiction with line 17.

**Alternative:** Instead of loosening the failure-mode test, tighten line 17 to remove the carve-out — i.e., treat convergence on the conventional answer as inherently suspect and require the delivered output to show more than assertion that "fundamentals forced it there" (e.g., require citing which specific list (a) fact makes each rejected alternative impossible). This keeps the outcome-based bar in line 21 but raises the evidentiary bar for claiming a legitimate convergence.
